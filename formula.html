Northwestern Formula Racing


Formula SAE is an intercollegiate competition where students design, build, and compete using an F1 style open wheel racecar. Northwestern Formula Racing brings this competetive environment to the students at Northwestern University. Over my 3 years on the team I have worked primarily with the powertrain and electronics subteams, where we are responsible for making the car go fast without blowing up or tearing itself apart.

Electronics


The only thing separating our cars from go-karts are their sophisticated electronics and controls (but don't tell the other subteams that). The wiring harness, sensors, communication network, and control logic represent a large portion of my contribution to the team. The process involves designing and updating the wiring harness to include important sensors, control systems, and actuators, continues with building and testing of individual modules, and concludes with assembly of the complete system. These modules include electro-pnuematic shifting, a multifunction steering wheel, power distribution, data acquisition, and an engine sensor/ECU harness.

Electronics Projects

Cockpit Controls

A multifunction steering wheel that displays telemetry through an LCD display, provides shift paddles and shift lights for quicker shifting, a traction control setting dial, and buttons for managing launch control. All this is controlled by software running on a Teensy microcontroller embeded within the carbon fiber steering wheel.

A pneumatic shifting system allows millisecond shifts through full integration of pneumatic actuators, a communication network, and a controller that consists of a Teensy microcontroller. Programming optimizes shifts by cutting ignition on upshifts to bypass the clutch entirely and also offers autoshifts based on gearing calculations for faster acceleration times.

Electronics Projects

Data Acquisition and Sensors

To help other subteams properly validate their designs, a custom data acquisition unit was built. Featuring a communication port, accelerometers, thermocouple amplification, and a microcontroller, the data acquisition unit is able to log data from various sensors on the car and from the ECU in csv files for performing design validation after the car is built and ready for testing.

A myriad of sensors are available on the car. These include sensors to allow proper engine calibration: manifold air pressure, oil pressure, lambda, exhaust gas temperature, crank position, cam position, coolant temperature, and inlet air temperature. Sensors available for dynamics validation include: shock position, brake temperature, brake pressure, and accelerometers.

Electronics Projects

Power and Communication

With so many modules and sensors available on the vehicle, there needs to be a way for the components to both get power and talk to each other. The power requirement is met by a stable and efficient power distribution module that uses MOSFETs to control power delivery, fuses to protect sensitive components, and regulators to change voltages. Communication between the ECU, data acquisition, shifting controller, steering wheel, and sensors is accomplished through a communication bus running the CAN protocol.

Powertrain


The powertrain subteam is responsible for everything that makes the four stages of a four stroke engine work along with how the engine itself interacts with the rest of the car (drivetrain). Some of the projects I have worked on for powertrain include the intake design, gearing calculations, and especially calibration. My biggest role has been in the calibration area which involves a lengthy and iterative process of configuring the ECU with respect to ignition timing, fuel injector duty cycle, air pressure, and various other parameters from the variety of sensors available.
